Everything you should track on your GLP-1 journey — and why it matters

The GLP-1 Boom — And the Tracking Gap

Millions of people are now on GLP-1 medications like Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, and Zepbound. These drugs have been genuinely transformative for weight loss and blood sugar management. But here's the thing most people discover a few weeks in: the medication alone isn't the whole picture.

Your dose changes over time. Your body reacts differently at each stage. Side effects come and go. Your weight fluctuates. Your muscle mass may drop if you're not careful. And your doctor needs data to make good decisions about your treatment.

Without a tracking system, you're flying blind. And when you're injecting a medication weekly that fundamentally changes your appetite, metabolism, and body composition — flying blind isn't great.

Why Tracking Your GLP-1 Medication Matters

GLP-1 medications work on a titration schedule — you start at a low dose and gradually increase over weeks or months. Each dose escalation can bring new side effects, different appetite suppression levels, and varying rates of weight loss.

Here's what happens when you don't track:

  • You forget when you took your last dose. Was it Tuesday or Wednesday? Missing a dose or doubling up can cause problems.
  • You can't identify patterns. Did the nausea get worse after the dose increase, or was it that spicy meal?
  • Your doctor has no data. "I think I've lost some weight and sometimes feel nauseous" is not useful clinical information.
  • You lose muscle instead of fat. Without monitoring protein intake and body composition, you might hit your goal weight but lose significant lean muscle mass — the so-called "Ozempic face" and "Ozempic body."

Tracking turns guesswork into data. And data leads to better outcomes.

What You Should Be Tracking

1. Dose and Schedule

  • Current dose level (e.g., 0.25mg, 0.5mg, 1mg, 1.7mg, 2.4mg for Wegovy)
  • Injection date and time
  • Next scheduled dose
  • Dose escalation dates

This is the foundation. You need to know exactly where you are in your titration schedule and never miss or double a dose.

2. Weight and Body Measurements

  • Daily or weekly weight (same time, same conditions — morning, after bathroom, before eating)
  • Waist circumference (a better indicator of visceral fat loss than scale weight)
  • Optional: body fat percentage if you have a smart scale

Weight alone is misleading. You might plateau on the scale while losing inches. Or lose 10 pounds but half of it is muscle. Multiple measurements give the full picture.

3. Food and Protein Intake

  • Daily protein grams (this is critical — more on this below)
  • General food log (even rough tracking helps identify trigger foods for side effects)
  • Water intake (dehydration is a common issue on GLP-1s)

4. Side Effects

  • Type: nausea, constipation, fatigue, heartburn, dizziness, hair loss
  • Severity: mild, moderate, severe
  • Duration: how long it lasted
  • Timing: relation to dose day (day 1, day 2, etc.)

5. Injection Site

  • Location used (left abdomen, right abdomen, left thigh, right thigh, back of arm)
  • Any reactions (redness, swelling, lumps)

Injection Site Rotation: The Most Overlooked Detail

Most GLP-1 users don't think much about where they inject. They find a spot that doesn't hurt too much and stick with it — literally. This is a mistake.

Repeated injections in the same area can cause lipodystrophy — hardened lumps of fat tissue under the skin. These lumps aren't just cosmetic; they can affect how the medication absorbs, leading to inconsistent drug levels in your blood.

The rotation rule is simple:

  • Use a different site each week
  • Rotate through at least 3-4 areas: left abdomen, right abdomen, left thigh, right thigh
  • Within each area, move at least 1 inch from your last injection point
  • Keep a visual log so you always know where you went last time

A tracker that maps your injection sites takes the guesswork out of rotation entirely.

Protein Intake: The Silent Make-or-Break Factor

Here's the stat that should concern every GLP-1 user: studies show that up to 39% of weight lost on semaglutide can be lean muscle mass, not fat.

Losing muscle is bad for your metabolism, your strength, your appearance, and your long-term health. The primary defense? Adequate protein intake.

The general recommendation for GLP-1 users is:

  • Minimum: 0.7g of protein per pound of body weight daily
  • Ideal: 1.0g per pound of body weight, especially if you're exercising
  • Challenge: GLP-1 medications suppress appetite aggressively, making it hard to eat enough

Practical tips:

  • Prioritize protein at every meal (eat it first before you fill up)
  • Use protein shakes to supplement on low-appetite days
  • Greek yogurt, eggs, chicken, and cottage cheese are your best friends
  • Don't skip meals entirely — even small protein-rich snacks matter

Side Effects: Knowing What's Normal vs. What's Not

Most GLP-1 side effects are manageable and temporary, especially in the first weeks of a new dose. But some warrant a call to your doctor.

Common (usually manageable):

  • Nausea (especially days 1-3 after injection)
  • Constipation
  • Mild fatigue
  • Reduced appetite (that's… the point)
  • Mild injection site reactions

Talk to your doctor if you experience:

  • Severe, persistent nausea or vomiting
  • Sharp abdominal pain (especially upper right — could signal gallbladder issues)
  • Signs of pancreatitis (severe abdominal pain radiating to the back)
  • Significant hair loss
  • Mental health changes (depression, anxiety)
  • Symptoms of hypoglycemia (shakiness, confusion, sweating)

By logging side effects daily, you can show your doctor exactly what you experienced, when, and how severe it was. This leads to better clinical decisions.

What to Bring to Your Doctor Appointments

When you walk into your GLP-1 check-up with data, you get a fundamentally different appointment. Instead of vague recollections, you bring:

  • Weight trend over the past month (not just today's number)
  • Side effect log correlated with dose changes
  • Protein intake averages (your doctor will be impressed)
  • Injection site rotation history
  • Questions based on patterns you've noticed

Doctors operate on data. The more you give them, the better they can manage your treatment.
